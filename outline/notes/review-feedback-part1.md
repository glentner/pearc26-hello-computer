# Review Feedback: Part 1

## Section: Abstract

### [FB-1.01] "Love letter and cautionary tale" phrasing
- **Type**: word-choice
- **Severity**: should-fix
- **Quote/context**: Ashish flags "equal parts love letter and cautionary tale" — "I understand it. And I liked it. But that depends upon who's reading it. If they get it and they don't find it cheesy, this is perfect."
- **Action**: Rephrase. Keep the energy of genuine excitement + caution but find a less overwrought formulation. Alternatives to workshop: "equal parts enthusiasm and vigilance," "written with conviction and caveats," or simply drop the label and let tone speak for itself.

### [FB-1.02] Em-dash overuse in abstract
- **Type**: tone
- **Severity**: should-fix
- **Quote/context**: Geoffrey notes two em dashes in the abstract alone. "I am conflicted about the em-dashes — because there's no point trying to hide it... The fact that agents have a tendency to use them a lot."
- **Action**: Audit em dashes across the entire paper. Reduce frequency — keep only those that are genuinely the best punctuation choice. The golden rule: "nobody cares until they notice."

### [FB-1.03] Overly flowery/clever language — general
- **Type**: tone
- **Severity**: should-fix
- **Quote/context**: Ashish: "after a certain point, these puns or these clever rephrasings start to get you — you're like, 'OK, I get it.'" Geoffrey agrees the agent "turned it up to 11." Discussion of the GRE-vocabulary effect and "amplification" by agents.
- **Action**: Keep the pop-culture section titles (they're intentional and thematic) but reduce inline cleverness — similes, overly artful phrases. Apply the golden rule: if the reader notices the style over the content, trim it.

### [FB-1.04] Keep puns and pop-culture references
- **Type**: meta
- **Severity**: consider
- **Quote/context**: Geoffrey is firm: "Let them hate it. And you know what? Let them hate it. And it's important — I want to keep it. Not the love letter part, but the puns." The puns are self-aware, thematic, and part of the paper's identity.
- **Action**: Retain all section-title puns (Shall We Play a Game, Mostly Harmless, Tea Earl Grey Hot, I Know Kung Fu, The Answer is 42, Don't Cross the Streams, End of Line). The puns stay; inline cleverness gets trimmed.

## Section: Introduction — "Mostly Harmless"

### [FB-1.10] "Mostly Harmless" argument misalignment
- **Type**: framing
- **Severity**: must-fix
- **Quote/context**: Geoffrey: "'mostly harmless' means mostly harmless. It doesn't mean 'well, they're gonna use this loaded gun whether we ask them to or not.'" The argument (users will do it anyway → engage) doesn't match the "mostly harmless" framing (cautious optimism, the tools themselves are largely safe).
- **Action**: Realign the subsection. "Mostly harmless" should convey: the tools themselves are mostly safe — in fact, frontier AI is arguably more reliable than unsupervised graduate students. The argument for engagement is that these tools are good, not that resistance is futile.

### [FB-1.11] Dog-fooding angle missing
- **Type**: content
- **Severity**: must-fix

- **Action**: Add dog-fooding argument to "Mostly Harmless." Frame: HPC staff should adopt these tools themselves to gain visceral understanding. Don't be the ostrich — engage or risk being outpaced by colleagues and users who are running circles around you.

### [FB-1.12] "Agentic assistance" too weak — needs "agent-first" framing
- **Type**: framing
- **Severity**: must-fix
- **Quote/context**: Geoffrey (from abstract discussion): the paper says "agentic assistance" but the process was agent-first — not proofreading, not one-shotting. "We need a stronger statement."
- **Action**: Replace all occurrences of "agentic assistance" (abstract, intro, conclusion) with language conveying agent-first, comprehensive workflow — not just AI helping with editing.
